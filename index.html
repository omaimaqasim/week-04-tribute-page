Poet Of East

ALLAMA MUHAMMAD IQBAL

1877 – 1938

"Nations are born in the hearts of poets, they prosper and die in the hands of politicians."

BIOGRAPHY

Early Life

Allama Muhammad Iqbal was born on 9th November 1877 in Sialkot, Punjab (British India), into a Kashmiri family. His father, Sheikh Noor Muhammad, was a devout Muslim of humble means, and his mother, Imam Bibi, was a kind-hearted woman deeply respected for her piety.

Iqbal received his early education in Sialkot, where he studied Arabic and Persian. Later, he joined Government College Lahore, where he was influenced by Sir Thomas Arnold, a professor of philosophy, who greatly shaped his intellectual development.

Iqbal’s birthplace in Sialkot

Education

After Lahore, Iqbal went to Europe for higher studies. He studied at the University of Cambridge and then earned a doctorate in philosophy from the University of Munich, Germany. His exposure to Western philosophy enriched his thought while strengthening his vision for Islamic revival.

He also studied law at Lincoln’s Inn in London, which gave him insight into modern political systems. During his stay in Europe, he engaged with the works of Western thinkers such as Nietzsche and Goethe. These experiences helped him develop a unique synthesis of Eastern spirituality and Western philosophy.

Cambridge University, where Iqbal studied

Poetry and Philosophy

Iqbal’s poetry, written in Urdu and Persian, inspired Muslims to rediscover their identity. Works such as Bang-e-Dra, Asrar-e-Khudi, and Payam-e-Mashriq reflect his ideas on selfhood, faith, and the awakening of nations.

His concept of “Khudi” (selfhood) encouraged individuals to realize their inner potential. He believed that true freedom came from spiritual awakening and self-respect. Through his verses, he connected classical Islamic values with the challenges of modern times.

Iqbal’s poetry inspired generations

Political Role

In his famous Allahabad Address of 1930, Iqbal envisioned a separate homeland for Muslims of India. His political philosophy guided the Pakistan Movement, earning him the title “Mufakkir-e-Pakistan” (Thinker of Pakistan).

He was a member of the All India Muslim League and actively participated in political debates. His speeches and writings laid the intellectual foundation for Muslim unity in South Asia. Many leaders, including Muhammad Ali Jinnah, were influenced by his vision for a new nation.

Allahabad Address (1930)

Legacy

Iqbal passed away on 21st April 1938 in Lahore. Today, he is remembered as the “Poet of the East” and the “National Poet of Pakistan,” whose vision continues to inspire millions across the Muslim world.

His tomb beside the Badshahi Mosque in Lahore remains a place of reverence. Schools, universities, and institutions across Pakistan are named after him. His poetry is still studied to inspire new generations with hope, courage, and faith.

Noble Works - Timeline

1915

Asrar-e-Khudi

The Secrets of the Self – Iqbal’s first philosophical poetry book in Persian, emphasizing selfhood (Khudi).

1923

Payam-e-Mashriq

Message of the East – Written in Persian, it was a reply to Goethe’s “West-östlicher Divan.”

1932

Javid Nama

The Book of Eternity – Considered his spiritual masterpiece, inspired by Dante’s Divine Comedy.

1924

Bang-e-Dra

The Call of the Marching Bell – Urdu poetry collection inspiring Muslims of the subcontinent.

1935

Bal-e-Jibril

Gabriel’s Wing – One of his most popular Urdu works with themes of spirituality and revolution.

1936

Zarb-e-Kalim

The Blow of Moses – Called his “declaration of war” against the modern age’s moral decline.
